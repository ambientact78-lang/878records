Why We Built This

Traditional limiters do one thing: they look at the full output signal and clamp everything when it crosses the ceiling. That works — but it also means a transient hit in the sub-bass causes limiting across your mids and highs. A kick drum punches, and your vocals dip. A bass note blooms, and your cymbals get crushed. The solution has always been the same: compromise.

The 878 Adaptive Limiter was built to end that compromise. By splitting the signal into four frequency bands — Sub (20–80 Hz), Low (80–500 Hz), Mid (500–8k Hz), and Hi (8k–20k Hz) — each band is limited independently. A transient in the low end stays in the low end. Your mids breathe freely. Your highs stay crisp.

Linear Phase Crossovers

Most multiband processors use minimum-phase crossovers because they're cheap to compute. The trade-off is phase smearing — different frequencies arrive at the output at slightly different times, blurring transients and creating subtle but audible artifacts, especially noticeable on stereo bus material.

Every crossover in the 878 Adaptive Limiter uses linear phase FIR filtering. All frequencies pass through with identical phase relationships. Your stereo image stays intact, transients hit with full punch, and the crossovers are inaudible — exactly as they should be.

Adaptive Thresholds

Most limiters give you a single ceiling and let you figure out the threshold. The Adaptive Limiter analyzes the short-term level of each band in real time and sets the limiting threshold relative to its content — so a bass-heavy mix and a high-mid forward mix are both handled intelligently without manual per-band dialing.

The result is a limiter that feels reactive and musical rather than mechanical. It catches peaks before they become distortion, while leaving the dynamic character of your mix largely untouched.

Harmonic Exciter

Limiting removes micro-dynamics. The Harmonic Exciter section is the antidote — a high-quality saturation stage with Drive (0–10) and Mix controls that introduce controlled even and odd harmonics back into the signal. At low Drive settings it adds subtle air and presence. Push it further and it becomes a creative tool: warm saturation, analog grit, or aggressive bite depending on where you take it.
